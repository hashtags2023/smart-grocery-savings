How to Meal Plan on $50 a Week

Feeding yourself — or a family — on $50 a week sounds impossible until you see exactly how it's done. The secret isn't deprivation or eating the same thing every day. It's a simple planning system that eliminates waste, reduces impulse buying, and makes every dollar work harder. Here's the complete step-by-step guide.

💡 Who this works for: This guide is written for 1–2 people. For a family of four, double the budget to $100/week — the same principles apply and the per-person cost stays the same.

The 5-Step System

1

Check what you already have

Before planning anything, open your fridge, freezer, and pantry and take inventory. Most households have 3–5 meals worth of ingredients they've forgotten about. Building your week around what you already own is the fastest way to cut costs without spending a dollar.

Write down proteins, grains, canned goods, and vegetables that need to be used up. These become the foundation of your meal plan.

2

Plan 5 dinners, not 7

Planning every single meal leads to over-buying and food waste. Instead, plan 5 dinners and use leftovers for the other 2 nights. Most recipes make enough for 2 meals — cook once, eat twice. This alone can cut your grocery bill by 20–30%.

For breakfast and lunch, keep it simple and repeatable: oatmeal, eggs, sandwiches, or salads. These are cheap, fast, and don't require planning.

3

Build meals around cheap proteins

Protein is usually the most expensive part of a meal. The most budget-friendly options are eggs ($0.20–0.30 each), dried beans and lentils ($1–2/lb), canned tuna ($1–1.50/can), chicken thighs ($1.50–2.50/lb), and ground turkey ($3–4/lb). These are significantly cheaper than beef, salmon, or deli meat.

Plan 2–3 meals around legumes or eggs each week and you'll save $15–20 without noticing a quality difference.

4

Shop with a strict list — and don't deviate

Write your shopping list directly from your meal plan. Every item on the list serves a specific meal. If it's not on the list, don't buy it. Impulse purchases are the #1 reason grocery budgets fail — the average shopper spends 23% more when shopping without a list.

Tip: organize your list by store section (produce, proteins, dairy, pantry) to shop faster and avoid doubling back through tempting aisles.

The Key Takeaway

Meal planning on $50 a week isn't about eating less — it's about wasting less. The average American throws away $1,500 worth of groceries per year. A weekly meal plan with a strict shopping list eliminates most of that waste and puts that money back in your pocket.

Start with just one week. Plan 5 dinners, build a list, and stick to it. Most people who try it once never go back to shopping without a plan.
